🗺 Employability Roadmap

A guide to the activities, experiences and skills you can build at each stage of your degree — and how they map to the skills employers look for.

Every activity you do at university builds skills that employers value. This roadmap helps you see how your academic work, extracurricular activities and part-time experience all contribute to your employability — even when it does not feel like career preparation.
Year 1
Building Foundations
📚 Core psychology modules
Foundations of psychological theory, research methods, and academic writing give you the intellectual toolkit employers expect from graduates.
Critical thinkingAcademic writingResearch awareness
🤝 Join a student society
Psychology Society, volunteering groups, or any society with a committee role develops teamwork, organisation and leadership from the start.
TeamworkCommunicationInitiative
👔 Part-time or voluntary work
Any role working with people — retail, hospitality, care, tutoring — builds communication, resilience and commercial awareness that employers value.
CommunicationResilienceCommercial awareness
🔗 Set up a LinkedIn profile
Start building your professional presence early. Follow employers, connect with alumni, and begin exploring what careers look like in practice.
Professional identityNetworking
Year 2
Building Experience
📊 Research methods and statistics
Quantitative and qualitative methods, survey design, data analysis and research reporting are directly valued by employers in research, HR, market research and policy roles.
Data analysisSurvey designReport writing
🏆 Take on a leadership role
Society president, committee member, student rep, peer mentor or sports captain — any leadership role demonstrates initiative and people management.
LeadershipOrganisationResponsibility
👥 Peer mentoring or tutoring
Supporting other students builds communication, empathy and the ability to explain complex ideas clearly — all highly transferable skills.
EmpathyExplanation skillsPatience
🏢 Work experience or shadowing
Even one day of shadowing in a relevant setting gives you something concrete to discuss in interviews and helps you test your career interests.
Sector knowledgeProfessional awareness
Final Year
Demonstrating and Applying
📄 Dissertation
Your dissertation is the strongest evidence of your research and analytical skills. It demonstrates independent thinking, project management and the ability to communicate complex findings.
Independent researchProject managementAnalytical writing
💬 Presentations and group projects
Any module requiring presentations or collaborative work builds the communication and teamwork skills employers consistently rank as most important.
PresentationTeamworkAdaptability
📝 Graduate scheme applications
Start researching and applying to graduate schemes in second semester of Year 2 or early in final year — many close in November and December.
Self-awarenessWritten communicationInterview skills
🔍 BPS Student Ambassador
The BPS Student Ambassador Scheme builds professional identity, communication skills and engagement with the psychology community — excellent for CVs.
Professional engagementCommunicationLeadership
After Graduation
Entry-Level and Further Training Routes
🎓 Graduate-entry roles
Many careers in HR, research, marketing, public sector, charity, and consultancy are open directly on graduation. These are the entry points for most non-clinical career pathways.
Degree-level skillsWork experience
🌟 Postgraduate study
Some routes — occupational psychology, clinical psychology, sport psychology, forensic psychology — require a BPS-accredited Masters or doctorate before full practitioner status.
Specialist knowledgeResearch training
📈 Continued professional development
CIPD qualifications for HR, MRS membership for market research, and BPS chartership routes all support career progression after your first graduate role.
Professional qualificationsChartered status
